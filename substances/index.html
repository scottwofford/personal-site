Acute Drug Danger and Dependence, 2026

The Gable (2006) chart, updated with fentanyl, carfentanil, methamphetamine, oxycodone, alprazolam, GHB. Acute single-dose lethality only; chronic harm not measured.

Caveats

  1. Acute, not chronic. The chart shows single-dose lethality. Cancer, heart disease, lung disease, dependence severity, withdrawal, social harms, and chronic-use mortality are not measured. Alcohol and tobacco kill far more people than the chart suggests; most of those deaths are chronic.
  2. Population estimates, wide uncertainty. Real-world doses vary by tolerance, purity, route, polysubstance use, and individual physiology. Several substances on this chart have no safe dose at any level.
  3. Six substances are author estimates, not Gable's data: fentanyl, carfentanil, methamphetamine, oxycodone, alprazolam, GHB. Approximate.
  4. Polysubstance use dominates real-world risk. Most opioid deaths involve a benzodiazepine or alcohol; most benzodiazepine deaths involve an opioid or alcohol. Single-substance ratios miss the dominant cause.

X-axis: active dose / lethal dose, log scale. Lower means safer (lethal dose is many multiples of active dose). Y-axis: dependence potential, qualitative buckets from Gable.

Sources

Inspiration: Drug danger and dependence chart on Wikipedia, from Gable, R. S. (2006), "Acute toxicity of drugs versus regulatory status," in J. M. Fish (ed.), Drugs and Society: U.S. Public Policy, Rowman & Littlefield, pp. 149-162.

Methodology: Gable, R. S. (2004), "Comparison of acute lethal toxicity of commonly abused psychoactive substances," Addiction 99(6): 686-695. Wiley · PubMed · Gable's summary page.

Status: draft. Not linked from main site. Drafted 2026-05-09. Initial chart generated by Claude, reviewed adversarially. Known issues below remain unresolved.

Known issues

Items a pre-publish /devil critique flagged. The chart has not been re-plotted to address them.

  • Carfentanil. No published human therapeutic index. Should probably be removed or moved outside the data area as a scale-only annotation.
  • Alprazolam, Rohypnol. Single-agent oral benzodiazepines have a wider safety margin than plotted. Both overstated as acutely toxic.
  • Ketamine, mescaline, GHB, ephedra, oxycodone, pentobarbital. Various directional and decade-magnitude errors versus published numbers.
  • Heroin and morphine. Plotted as separate points. Heroin is a morphine prodrug; visual implies more independence than the pharmacology supports.
  • MDMA classification. Plotted as stimulant. Pharmacology is closer to entactogen with hallucinogenic properties.
  • Cannabis category. Single data point with its own category color. No methodological reason for separation from hallucinogens.
  • No uncertainty intervals. Single-point estimates over a three-decade log scale; the underlying literature reports ranges.
  • Dependence bands for the six added substances are qualitative and unsourced on the chart itself.

Why this is here

Personal curiosity. The Wikipedia version is widely cited but predates the fentanyl crisis and benzodiazepine prominence. I wanted to see how the format extends with current substances. Draft commentary on Gable's framework, not authoritative. If you find a better-sourced version, tell me and I'll replace this one.
