Acute Drug Danger and Dependence Chart, 2026

Updated version of the Gable (2006) chart with fentanyl, carfentanil, methamphetamine, oxycodone, alprazolam, and GHB added. Acute toxicity only; chronic harm is not measured.

Read this first

  1. Acute toxicity only. The chart shows single-dose lethality. It does not measure cancer risk, cardiovascular disease, lung disease, dependence severity, withdrawal danger, social harms, or chronic-use mortality. Alcohol and tobacco kill far more people than this chart suggests because most of those deaths are chronic.
  2. Population-level estimates with substantial uncertainty. Active and lethal doses in real-world use depend on tolerance, purity, route of administration, polysubstance use, and individual physiology. Do not use this chart to estimate a "safe" dose for any substance. There is no safe dose for several substances on it.
  3. Carfentanil's human therapeutic index is undetermined. The most acutely dangerous-looking substance on the chart has no characterized human therapeutic index. Its position is extrapolated from animal pharmacology and post-mortem toxicology. Treat it as a scale marker, not a data point.
  4. Six substances are author estimates, not Gable's data. Fentanyl, carfentanil, methamphetamine, oxycodone, alprazolam, and GHB were added to the Gable (2006) format using secondary literature. Treat them as approximate.
  5. Polysubstance use changes everything. Most opioid deaths involve a benzodiazepine or alcohol. Most benzodiazepine deaths involve an opioid or alcohol. Single-substance ratios do not capture the dominant real-world risk.

The chart

X-axis: active dose / lethal dose (log scale). Lower is safer (lethal dose is many multiples of active dose). Y-axis: dependence potential, qualitative buckets from Gable's framework.

Sources and methodology

Inspired by: Drug danger and dependence chart on Wikipedia, derived from Gable, R. S. (2006), "Acute toxicity of drugs versus regulatory status," in J. M. Fish (ed.), Drugs and Society: U.S. Public Policy, Rowman & Littlefield, pp. 149-162.

Methodology source: Gable, R. S. (2004), "Comparison of acute lethal toxicity of commonly abused psychoactive substances," Addiction 99(6): 686-695. Wiley · PubMed · Gable's summary page.

Page status: Standalone, not linked from the main site. Drafted 2026-05-09. Initial chart generated by Claude, reviewed adversarially. Placement-correction items below remain unresolved.

Adversarial review (issues unresolved)

A pre-publish critique flagged the following placement and methodology issues. They are listed here for transparency; the chart has not yet been re-plotted to address them.

  • Carfentanil. No published human active or lethal dose. Position is extrapolated from animal pharmacology and post-mortem cases with heavy polysubstance confounding. Should arguably be removed or moved outside the data area as a scale-only annotation.
  • Nicotine. Critic claims plotted position is too safe by roughly 0.6 decades versus Gable's data; should be near methamphetamine's safety ratio, not caffeine's.
  • Alprazolam and Rohypnol. Critic claims single-agent oral benzodiazepines have a much wider safety margin than plotted; current positions overstate acute toxicity.
  • Ketamine, mescaline, GHB, ephedra, oxycodone, pentobarbital. Various directional and decade-magnitude errors versus published numbers.
  • Heroin and morphine. Plotted as separate points; the visual reading suggests independent risk profiles, which is not the underlying pharmacology (heroin is a morphine prodrug).
  • MDMA classification. Plotted as a stimulant; pharmacology is closer to an entactogen with hallucinogenic properties.
  • Cannabis category. Single data point given its own category color despite no clear methodological reason for separation from hallucinogens.
  • No uncertainty intervals. Single-point estimates over a three-decade log scale are misleading; the underlying literature reports ranges.
  • Dependence-band placements for the six added substances are qualitative and unsourced inline on the chart itself.

Why this is here

Personal curiosity. The Wikipedia version of this chart is widely cited but predates the fentanyl-era opioid crisis and benzodiazepine prominence. I wanted to see how the format extends with current substances. The chart is published here as a draft commentary on the Gable framework, not as an authoritative reference. If you find a better-sourced version, please tell me and I will replace this one.
