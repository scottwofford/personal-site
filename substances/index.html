Acute Active/Lethal Dose Ratio and Dependence Potential of Psychoactive Drugs

Updated 2026 with fentanyl, carfentanil, methamphetamine, oxycodone, alprazolam, and GHB.

Read this before reading the chart.
  1. Acute toxicity only. The chart shows single-dose lethality. It does not measure cancer risk, cardiovascular disease, lung disease, dependence severity, withdrawal danger, social harms, or chronic-use mortality. Alcohol and tobacco kill far more people than the chart suggests because most of those deaths are chronic, not acute.
  2. Population-level estimates with substantial uncertainty. Active and lethal doses in real-world use depend on tolerance, purity, route of administration, polypharmacy, and individual physiology. Do not use this chart to estimate a "safe" dose for any substance. There is no safe dose for several substances on this chart.
  3. Carfentanil's human therapeutic index is undetermined. The most acutely-dangerous-looking substance on this chart has no characterized human therapeutic index. Position is estimated from animal pharmacology and post-mortem toxicology. Treat as scale-marker, not data point.
  4. Six substances are author estimates, not Gable's data. Fentanyl, carfentanil, methamphetamine, oxycodone, alprazolam, and GHB were added to the Gable (2006) format from secondary literature. Treat them as approximate.
  5. Polysubstance use changes everything. Most opioid deaths involve a benzodiazepine or alcohol. Most benzodiazepine deaths involve an opioid or alcohol. Single-substance ratios shown here do not capture the dominant real-world risk.

Adversarial review (issues flagged before publish, unresolved)

A pre-publish critique flagged the following placement and methodology issues. They are listed here for transparency; the chart itself has not yet been re-plotted to address them.
  • Carfentanil: No published human active or lethal dose. Plotted position is extrapolated from animal pharmacology + post-mortem cases with heavy polysubstance confounding. Should arguably be removed or moved outside the data area as a scale-only annotation.
  • Nicotine: Critic claims plotted position is too safe by ~0.6 decades vs Gable's data (should be near methamphetamine's safety ratio, not caffeine's).
  • Alprazolam: Critic claims single-agent oral alprazolam has a much wider safety margin than plotted; current position overstates acute toxicity.
  • Rohypnol (flunitrazepam): Same issue, smaller magnitude.
  • Ketamine, mescaline, GHB, ephedra, oxycodone, pentobarbital: Critic claims various directional and decade-magnitude errors vs Gable's published numbers.
  • Heroin/morphine: Plotted as separate points, but heroin is a morphine prodrug; visual reading suggests they are independent risk profiles, which is not the underlying pharmacology.
  • MDMA classification: Plotted as stimulant (yellow triangle); pharmacology is closer to entactogen with hallucinogenic properties.
  • Cannabis category: Single data point ("Marijuana") given its own color category despite no clear methodological reason for separation from hallucinogens.
  • No uncertainty intervals. Single-point estimates over a three-decade log scale are misleading; the underlying literature reports ranges.
  • Dependence-band placements for the six added substances are qualitative and unsourced inline.

Why this is here

Personal curiosity. The Wikipedia version of this chart is widely cited but predates the fentanyl-era opioid crisis and benzodiazepine prominence. I wanted to see how the format extends with current substances. The chart is published here as a draft commentary on the Gable framework, not as an authoritative reference. If you find a better-sourced version, please tell me and I'll replace this one.
